Chapter 6

Questions

Once Sam and Eric have claimed to see a beast, none of the boys think twice about the existence of the beast. The reason for the quick acceptance of the beast is because the beast is a form of personification to the fear and aggression the boys have started to develop. Additionally, the boys have not tried to verify the existence for one moment because of their loss of sense. As a result, the boys on the island have a power struggle; yearning to defeat or run from this beast.

On page 78, Simon says “I don’t believe in the beast” (Golding, 78), having the only reasonable reaction to this evident lacking claim Sam and Eric have. The cause of Simon’s doubtfulness of the beast is his different emotional state compared to the other boys. Additionally, the boys have neglected their physiological needs of security and relationships. Furthermore, Simon has achieved and surpassed his basic needs to the point where he has reached self-actualization through respecting the island and helping others. Along with this, Simon’s disbelief in the beast is significant showing that he is different from the group and maybe the savior of the children’s mental state.

As a result of the beast’s existence somewhat being confirmed, Ralph and Jack insist on going after the beast. However, their intentions differ. On one hand, Ralph wants to prove there is no threat to the island; contrasting his leadership style of people-pleasing. On the other hand, Jack wants to confirm the beast and kill it displaying the protection he will provide. Despite these Jacks remarks, he of them are in fear himself: “[Ralph] heard Jacks whisper, ‘Scared?’” (Golding, 92).
